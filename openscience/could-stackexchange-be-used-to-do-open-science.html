Open Science Stack Exchange is a question and answer site for people interested in making scientific research, data and dissemination accessible to all. It's 100% free, no registration required.

Could StackExchange be used to “do” open science?

up vote 10 down vote favorite
5

The Q&A sites on StackExchange are a great platform for gathering knowledge about a multitude of things.

In my eyes, the reputation system does an excellent job when it comes to rating questions as well as answers according to usefulness and objectivity of their content.

Thus, the question arises, whether this framework along with the rating system could not be used beyond Q&A? In fact, I have been playing with this idea for a while but to be honest, I still could not make up my mind about it:

Could StackExchange's Q&A framework be used for A&R, as in Article and Review?

When looking at the tools one has at hand for writing a Question: There is the possibility to write equations (see mathematics), images are supported, links, ... everything you need to write an entire article, and the same goes for an answer (a review in A&R).

Just as a question and its answers are rated now, an article and its reviews could be rated (maybe a bad rating without leaving a comment should not be allowed in A&R). The history function for the edits already serves as version control and would assure a complete history of an article and all of its reviews. The rating system would serve as measure for impact-factor with the additional advantage that reviewing articles could finally be rewarded. The only thing that would need some adaptation is the accept answer functionality, as rather than a review, the article would need to be accepted at some point.

In fact, some time ago I started a proposition about this on area51, just to see what other users think about it. It seems that some really like it and others think the idea is plain stupid. As I said, I'm not really decided yet, I think such a site bears a great potential to publish, review, judge and access scientific findings openly.

So the question at hand really is:

What are the arguments in favour or against using StackExchange's Q&A framework as a ScienceExchange A&R platform to publish scientific results openly?

It is true, that the StackExchange software would perfectly fit the purpose of publicly peer-reviewing research-papers in real-time. And indeed, for physics some people have tweeked an SE-clone software to create a platform for doing almost exactly what this question suggests.

However, online public peer-reviewing potentially involves a lot of lengthy discussions. Therefore even though the StackExchange software would be very convenient to use, it is unclear if the StackExchange company would support an SE site dedicated to public scientific peer-reviewing, as extensive discussions are more or less strongly (depending on the specific site) discouraged in the whole network.

I was wondering that myself - thanks for posting! I followed the link to your ScienceExchange proposal and liked what I saw there. I also signed up, even though I am not sure we'd actually need yet another site - that functionality might well fit in here.

As mentioned over there, I imagine the system to work slightly differently from the A & R approach that you outlined:

  • the question functionality would be used to publicly post research questions of the kind normally laid out non-publicly in grant proposals;
  • the answer functionality would be for posting any information addressing that question, by anyone (not just the original poster), and through repeated (e.g. daily) use of the "Add Another Answer" button, you'd get basically lab notebook functionality (in a collaborative flavour), which might spur additional (research) questions that may receive answers on the way to finding an answer for the first one;
  • comments could then serve as peer reviews (including from potential funders for next steps) at any stage on the way;
  • we could have an overlay journal (perhaps on meta) that would list postings that meet some additional criteria defined by its editorial policies (e.g. at least two positive reviews with a score of five or above, or some such).

I do think it could work. However, I'll take a contrary position to flush out some issues: reputation (in the SE sense) isn't a suitable proxy for peer review.

  1. It's a denominator issue. Reputation is impossibly correlated with audience appeal. In an extreme example, let the audience be 95% quantitative environmental scientists and 5% theoretical physicists. It's not hard to imagine each group might feel uncomfortable reviewing the other's contributions. The physicists will be the "low-rep" users. Possible solutions: separate sites by denominator, balance the pools, or have normed reputation scales.
  2. It's a selection issue. Denominator issues aside, when reviewers get to choose what they'll review it lends to a degree of bias. This exists in traditionally peer-reviewed journals as well, although editors are able to mitigate it somewhat. Possible solutions: some degree of review management or random reviewer assignment.
  3. It's a skill issue. All reviewers are not helpfully comparable, and everyone gets an equal chance to cast a vote on a SE question. While many editors value a diversity of opinion, they do strive to ensure a threshold skill set. Possible solutions: management or use of the comments section.

All said, one might deemphasize the "reputation" function and emphasize the "comments" function.
