Why do some SoCs have a separate power management IC?

up vote 9 down vote favorite

Some Systems-on-Chips have a dedicated separate power management IC:

  • Power Management for i.MX Application Processors
  • Automotive Power Management IC (PMIC) for ARM Cortex A15 processors

Why is this function not integrated on the SoC directly?

There are quite a few reasons to choose not to put a Power Management IC (PMIC for short) in a SoC.

One such reason could be heat, if the SoC drains enough current the PMIC will create heat because of that. If that PMIC then is inside the same die as the SoC that means the SoC itself will have a lot of extra heat production on the inside, which can mean it can't do as much processing.

Another reason is simply space. To have a good PMIC you need reasonably decent transistors for a stable output, which take a lot more room than simple digital transistor stages for the SoC itself, so a PMIC would waste a lot of valuable space with comparatively few transistors.

Coupled to that there's the cost of an accurate process: If you then have a 22nm process to make the main SoC, but have 220nm features in the PMIC, all the space in the PMIC has been made on a 10x too accurate process, which makes it much more expensive than just doing it on a 180nm or 220nm process separately in the first place.

A third reason can be the application areas. You link an Automotive PMIC from TI, this is an excellent example. Automotive has a lot of spikes and mess and the PMIC would need quite high qualifications to keep that out of the digital stuff. If you then put the PMIC in the chip, that kind of only increases the risk of problems. Plus, if the applications are wide and many, you may need different PMICs for different solutions, making the internal PMIC only useful in 10 to 30% of the cases. Which again links back to the waste of space.

Usually PMICs are only included if the main die technology is "cheap enough" to waste the space and the main processor doesn't generate any real heat to mention.
