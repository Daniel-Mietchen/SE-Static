Are there specific engineering advantages of big-endian and little-endian systems?

up vote 8 down vote favorite
2

Big-endian systems and little-endian systems have been there for a very long time. And apparently, there still seems to be no universal convergence on it.

From broadly what one finds is that they both now exists partly because of old legacy and they both originated due to specific engineering choices made in the past for specific processors. As wiki puts it

Both forms of endianness are in widespread use in digital electronic engineering. The initial choice of endianness of a new design is often arbitrary, but later technology revisions and updates perpetuate the existing endianness and many other design attributes to maintain backward compatibility.

It surely makes lower level language codes painful.

There are many sources who explains what they are but there must have been specific engineering advantages why either of them got selected in the earliest generations of processors.

What are the specific distinct engineering advantages that big endian has over little endian and vice-versa?

Little-endian has the advantage that performing piecewise calculations on multi-byte values (eg. adding two 32-bit numbers one byte at a time) is easier because accessing each subsequent piece involves a pointer increment -- typically a very fast operation. This is particularly true for bit-serial computers, which operate on one bit at a time.

Big-endian has the advantage that it's easier for humans to reason about, because it matches how numbers are written on paper.

The little-endian architecture also has advantages in terms of human perception and understanding. For example, if bn is the value of the byte at address n, it seems more intuitive to some that the value of the 32-bit word at address n (consisting of bytes n, n+1, n+2, and n+3) should be

bn        + bn+1×256  + bn+2×2562 + bn+3×2563

i.e.,

bn+0×2560 + bn+1×2561 + bn+2×2562 + bn+3×2563

In big-endian systems, the exponent goes down as the address goes up.

A similar effect applies to the numbering of bits.  If you know that a word on a little-endian system is all zeroes, except bit n is set (to 1), then you know that the value of the word is 2n.  Given the same information in a big-endian system, you need to ask how many bits there are in the word, and subtract n from that, to evaluate the word.  And, while this might not directly affect programming, if you number your bits in the little-endian way on a big-endian system, you'll get chaos.
